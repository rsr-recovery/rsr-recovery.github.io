Beyond Walking: Generalized Frequency-Adaptive Learning for Versatile and Robust Legged Locomotion

Our method make use of Fourier transform in the RL pipeline to train legged controllers with robust and versatile locomotion skills beyond walking.

Abstract

     Legged animals can navigate complex terrains by deliberately selecting discrete footholds and adjusting kinematic configuration to proactively adapt to the environment. Similarly, legged robots can leverage this inherent capability, endowing them with great potential in real-world robotic applications that require high mobility. Executing demanding tasks in such challenging environments requires legged controllers with robust and versatile locomotion skills far beyond basic walking. However, existing studies mainly focus on developing simplistic walking controllers tailored for specific settings, failing to achieve adaptability and generalization across diverse complex tasks and outdoor dynamic environments.
     For this reason, we propose generalized frequency-adaptive learning, an integrated learning and control framework that augments RL-based imitation with Fourier transform and multi-constraint optimization. Inspired by Fourier transform, a spectrum convolution module is introduced to capture the frequency spectrum representation of the environment and the onboard state. Subsequently, we develop an exteroceptive Fourier transformer and a proprioceptive Fourier transformer to adapt the network actions to the surroundings and enhance the controller bandwidth.
     The result is a generalized legged controller characterized by remarkable robustness and versatility, applicable to various robots and tasks. We rigorously evaluate the controller on quadruped, biped, and humanoid robots in diverse challenging simulated and real-world tasks. It outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both the simulated dojo and the real-world multi-task comparison, completing a long-distance campus hike with human-level agility and winning the remote-control track of the IEEE RAS Quadruped Robot Challenges at IROS2024.

METHODOLOGY

     The goal of this work is to develop a universal control framework for diverse legged robots to endow them with robust and versatile locomotion skills to execute various challenging real-world tasks.
     To achieve this goal, we first develop a hybrid learning mechanism, in which the model-free policy is trained to imitate kinematically and dynamically consistent reference from the model-based generator through a barrier-style reward formulation, facilitating efficient policy iteration and acquisition of versatile locomotion skills. The control policy is then guided by Fourier Online Adaption (FOA), a frequency-aware adaptation strategy that dynamically adjusts the policy to accommodate external environment changes and internal state variations. The adaptive strategy equips the policy with proactive robustness to the surrounding environment and enhanced control bandwidth of the policy output, which are crucial for real-world applications. Finally, the networks are optimized through the Constrained Concurrent Teacher-Student (C2TS) algorithm, which enforces multiple physical constraints to ensure safe policy performance. By leveraging the privileged training paradigm, the algorithm achieves robust locomotion in unstructured environments within limited time budgets.

EXPERIMENTS

Implementation & Deployment

Our controller is trained in IsaacGym simulator with massively parallelism. The training process takes about 1.5 hours of wall-clock time on an NVIDIA RTX 3090, Then, the trained controller is directly deployed on the real hardware without any fine-tuning. The locomotion control policy runs at 50Hz on hardware, and the low-level joint PD controller runs at 1kHz in parallel.

Fourier Adaptivity

To demonstate the effectiveness of Fourier online adaptation, we analyze the acquired frequency-domain features of EFT (Exteroceptive Fourier Transformer), and then study how PFT (Proprioceptive Fourier Transformer) enhances the control bandwidth.
The results shows that the EFT is capable of capturing the key features of different terrains and providing sufficient information for the controller to achieve proactive adaptiveness during legged locomotion. Through comparison, we find that the PFT can effectively enhance the control bandwidth of the controller, empowering the robot to achieve smoother and more stable locomotion in complex environments.
