Overview

Abstract

     Legged animals can self-right and stand up from diverse postures across various environments. Empowering legged robots with animal-like recovery capabilities expands their real-world applications. However, most existing recovery controllers, which rely on predefined trajectories, are limited to flat terrain. Additionally, current learning-based policies produce control outputs that are either overly aggressive or sluggish, both of which lead to unsatisfactory hardware performance.
     To bridge the gap, this paper proposes a novel real-to-sim-to-real learning framework that enables legged robots to acquire both agile and robust recovery skills. Unlike previous learning approaches that train robots from scratch, we employ adversarial imitation learning with hardware-collected demonstrations to promote natural motions and enhance energy efficiency. For robust recovery on complex terrains, a terrain imagination module is integrated to predict key terrain properties via onboard sensing. To ensure reliable sim-to-real transfer, the recovery policy is trained with domain randomization, terrain curriculum learning, and geometry decomposition techniques.
     The trained policy is directly deployed on the Unitree Go2. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that a small-sized quadruped has demonstrated advanced recovery skills across diverse environments, outperforming prior studies with more powerful hardware.

Implementation & Deployment

The quadruped robot is trained in the IsaacGym simulator with massively parallel training. The training process takes 2 hours on an NVIDIA RTX 3090, spanning 5,000 iterations. The trained policy can be directly deployed on the Unitree Go2 hardware without any fine-tuning. Sensor readings and policy actions are read and sent via ROS2 and the Unitree SDK2.
